When Sentiment Lies: Building the First Arabic Sarcasm Dataset

May 1, 2020

2020   ·   arabic-nlp   sarcasm   sentiment-analysis   dataset     ·   research  

Run any Arabic tweet through a decent sentiment classifier and it will tell you whether the author sounds happy, unhappy, or neutral. The tools have gotten good at this. They’ve been trained on thousands of labelled examples, fine-tuned on Arabic-specific language models, benchmarked across multiple datasets. For a lot of tweets, they work.

Then you try it on something like: “يا سلام، الخدمة كانت ممتازة، ما انتظرنا غير ساعتين” — “The service was excellent, we only waited two hours.” Any Arabic speaker reads that as frustration in a thin disguise. The word for excellent is doing the opposite of its literal job. The classifier outputs: positive.

That is the sarcasm problem. It is not exotic. It is everywhere.

Arabic, sarcasm, and a missing dataset

Sarcasm is woven into how Arabic speakers communicate online. It is how people complain about a bad experience without quite saying so. It is how they vent about traffic, delays, and Monday mornings in a way that lands as funny rather than bitter. Walk through Arabic Twitter on any given day and sarcasm is not a rhetorical device — it is the dominant mode.

Arabic NLP had made meaningful progress on sentiment analysis by 2019. Several datasets existed, benchmarks had been established, and transformer-based models trained on Arabic were beginning to appear. What did not exist was an Arabic sarcasm dataset. Not a public one. Not a benchmark. Nothing that would let a researcher train a model or measure how well it performed.

This was a peculiar gap, because sarcasm and sentiment analysis are not independent problems. Sarcasm is, among other things, a mechanism for saying the opposite of what you mean. A sentence that looks positive is actually negative. If your sentiment tool cannot detect sarcasm, it is not just missing sarcasm — it is actively getting sentiment wrong on a non-trivial fraction of the data.

The approach: re-annotating what already existed

Rather than starting from scratch, I went back to what was already there. Two existing Arabic sentiment datasets — the SemEval 2017 Arabic dataset and ASTD — had been carefully assembled and labelled. The tweets were already collected, already organised, already paired with sentiment labels. The question was: what happens if you add a sarcasm label on top?

Re-annotating existing data had a specific advantage: it produced paired labels. The same tweet, with the same sentiment label from the original annotation, now also had a sarcasm label. This meant we could look directly at the interaction between the two — not in the abstract, but across every tweet in the dataset.

The resulting dataset was ArSarcasm: 10,547 tweets, each labelled for sarcasm (binary), sentiment (positive/negative/neutral), and dialect (Egyptian, Levantine, Gulf, Maghrebi, Modern Standard Arabic).

What the data showed

The first number that came out of the analysis was 16%. One in every six tweets in these sentiment datasets was sarcastic. That is not a rare edge case. It is a routine feature of how people write in Arabic on social media. The expectation going in had been that sarcasm would be a minority phenomenon — present but marginal. It was not marginal.

The second finding was about what sarcasm does to sentiment. When you look at sarcastic tweets and ask what sentiment they actually express — what the author meant, not what the surface form suggests — the distribution flips sharply. Tweets that look positive are, in the majority of sarcastic cases, actually negative in intent. The surface form and the intended meaning run in opposite directions.

The practical consequence of this is measurable. Sentiment classifiers trained and evaluated on datasets that ignore sarcasm are being tested on flawed gold labels — because some of those gold labels reflect the surface form of sarcastic tweets rather than their intended meaning. When you separate the sarcastic tweets from the rest and look at how sentiment models perform on each group, the gap is significant. Sarcasm is not just noise. It is structured noise that pulls in a specific direction.

Annotating subjectivity

Annotating sarcasm is not like annotating named entities or part-of-speech tags. There is no ground truth in the same sense. Whether you read a sentence as sarcastic depends on who you are, what you know, what culture you inhabit, and whether you share context with the author. The annotators in this dataset were labelling other people’s words — perceived sarcasm, in the terminology the field later adopted — which means their judgements are genuine but not authoritative.

The inter-annotator agreement reflects this. It is lower than you would see on more clear-cut annotation tasks, and that is not a failure of the process — it is an honest property of the task. Sarcasm is genuinely ambiguous in ways that many NLP labels are not.

This turned out to be the most interesting design question raised by the dataset: if perceived sarcasm is noisy because annotators can miss the author’s intent, what would it look like to collect sarcasm that authors label themselves? That question pointed directly to the next stage of the work — but that is a different story.

What a model can do with it

A BiLSTM trained on ArSarcasm as a baseline achieved an F1-sarcasm score of 0.46. That number looks low, and by the standards of saturated benchmarks it is. But sarcasm detection on a genuinely hard, multilingual, dialectally diverse dataset with noisy labels has a very different difficulty profile than, say, part-of-speech tagging. The 0.46 was never meant to represent a solution — it was a stake in the ground, a starting point for the community to build from.

The dataset is publicly available at github.com/iabufarha/ArSarcasm, and the paper is at the ACL Anthology.

What came next

ArSarcasm-v2 extended the dataset to 15,548 tweets — better dialectal coverage, cleaner distribution — and became the basis for the WANLP 2021 Shared Task on Sarcasm and Sentiment Detection in Arabic.

But the more lasting consequence of building ArSarcasm was what it revealed about the sentiment tools that already existed. Those tools had been trained and tested on datasets where 16% of the labels were quietly wrong — not through carelessness, but because no one had looked for sarcasm there. The datasets assumed sincerity. Arabic speakers online do not. Once you know the gap is there, you cannot unsee it — and fixing it, rather than working around it, became the next several years of work.
